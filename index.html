The Communication Avalanche

The number of scientific articles published every year is immense and exceeds a million publications annually by some estimates. While we are all under pressure to communicate our research in written form (be it for an article, a report, or a thesis) we often struggle to receive the feedback we need when we need it: We are all hard-pressed to read, write, and communicate -- but with limited amount of time and a busy schedule hardly anyone can afford to make an effort and review our colleagues' work in a timely manner. The communication avalanche creates frustrating questions for all of us: Who will see and read my work? Who will have the time to disseminate critically my work and provide me with constructive feedback?

The Isolated Communicator in an Interdisciplinary Field

We are all aware that the classical organization of scientific fields is challenged by new interdisciplinary or convergent approaches to research questions. Today, we are expected to combine approaches and techniques from a host of scientific disciplines. Thomas Kalil (White House Office of Science and Technology Policy) states: "[...] the ideal scientist working in the convergence model [...][is] shaped like a letter T. The top part of the T symbolizes breadth of interests and knowledge, while the stem represents depth of expertise in one area.".

While interdisciplinary research is generally regarded as an asset, it can also cause frustration: When working on our niche problem within a larger interdisciplinary problem, we sometimes need critical feedback on a piece of communication that is too technical for our colleagues to understand thoroughly. Applying techniques from research fields that are new to us is another challenge as it is hard to be aware of all assumptions and implications that may be obvious to someone familiar with the field.

The CrowdReview Solution

Our CrowdReview solution helps you both stem the communication avalanche and connect with researchers that can provide thorough in-depth feedback on your work. Reviewing through CrowdReview is asynchronous (you review at your own pace), open (learn from the work and reviews of others), and open to all disciplines (immerse yourself in the technical aspects of other disciplines). You gain experience by communicating your research to a wide audience, receiving critical feedback from others in the field, and reviewing the work of other communicators.

FAQ

Will I not get scooped writing about my ongoing research on CrowdReview?
In theory this is possible and you should decide for yourself if and what to post on CrowdReview. In practice, you will find that the people that have the potential to publish on your ongoing research before you do will be close to you in your field -- and are therefore likely to be aware of what you are working on anyway. As an individual researcher you need to balance multiple risk factors when it comes to this question: If you do not communicate your research at all until your paper or thesis is finished you may have minimized your risk of being scooped (although this may still happen) but you may be entirely unknown in your field (not to speak of adjacent and far-flung communities). If you do communicate your research at an early stage you may increase your chances of being scooped but you will also greatly improve your visibility. Greater visbility in research can result in better contacts for future research, and improved job prospects. The scientific community is abound with successful examples of open scientific research, such as Carl Boettinger's Open Lab Notebook , which "has seen six projects go from conception to publication" over the span of approximately three years. Don't dread the scoop -- dread irrevelance.
Will I retain ownership of the content I post on CrowdReview?
You will retain all ownership and copyright of your work. When posting your work on CrowdReview you will be able to choose the license you want to distribute your work under. A wide range of licenses is provided by Creative Commons .
